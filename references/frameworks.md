# Strategic Analysis Frameworks Reference

## Framework Selection Guide

| Framework | Best When | Key Questions It Answers |
|-----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| SWOT | General strategic assessment; good starting point | What are our strengths/weaknesses? What opportunities/threats exist? |
| Porter's Five Forces | Industry structure matters; competitive intensity | How attractive is this industry? Where does power lie? |
| PESTLE | Macro environment shifting (regulation, tech, demographics) | What external forces shape this industry? |
| Value Chain Analysis | Case hinges on operational efficiency or cost | Where is value created/destroyed in operations? |
| BCG Matrix | Multi-business portfolio decisions | Which business units deserve investment? |
| Ansoff Matrix | Growth strategy decisions | Should we grow via new markets, new products, or both? |
| VRIO | Evaluating sustainable competitive advantage | Is this advantage valuable, rare, inimitable, organized? |
| Blue Ocean | Market creation vs. competing in existing space | Can we create uncontested market space? |
| Game Theory | Competitor response matters (pricing wars, entry) | How will competitors react to our moves? |
| Financial Ratio Analysis | Case includes financial statements | Is this company financially healthy? |

## How to Apply Frameworks at A+ Level

The difference between a B and an A+ analysis is insight density.

**B-level (avoid):**
> "Strength: strong brand. Weakness: high costs. Opportunity: emerging markets."

This is generic — it could describe any company. No case facts, no quantification, no insight.

**A-level (aim for):**
> "The brand commands a 15% price premium in mature markets (Exhibit 3), but this premium has eroded from 22% over five years — suggesting the moat is real but narrowing. Meanwhile, the cost structure (Exhibit 5) shows 40% of COGS tied to a single supplier, creating both a weakness (supplier power) and a strategic imperative (vertical integration or diversification)."

### Rules for A+ Framework Application

1. **Ground every point in case facts** — reference exhibits, financials, quotes
2. **Quantify wherever possible** — "revenue grew 12%" beats "revenue grew"
3. **Connect across frameworks** — a SWOT weakness should echo a Porter's force
4. **Flag inferences** — "This suggests..." or "Based on the 15% decline, we can infer..."
5. **Prioritize within each framework** — not all SWOT items are equally important; rank them

## SWOT Deep Dive

### Structure
```
| Internal | External |
|----------|----------|
| Strengths | Opportunities |
| Weaknesses | Threats |
```

### Common Mistakes
- Listing too many items (pick 3-5 per quadrant, prioritized)
- Confusing internal/external (brand reputation = internal; market growth = external)
- Vague language ("good management" — what specifically?)

## Porter's Five Forces Deep Dive

### The Five Forces
1. **Threat of New Entrants** — barriers to entry, capital requirements, brand loyalty, regulation
2. **Bargaining Power of Suppliers** — concentration, switching costs, forward integration threat
3. **Bargaining Power of Buyers** — concentration, price sensitivity, switching costs
4. **Threat of Substitutes** — price-performance of alternatives, switching costs
5. **Competitive Rivalry** — number/size of competitors, industry growth, differentiation, exit barriers

### Rating Scale
Rate each force as Low / Medium / High with supporting evidence. Then synthesize: "Overall, this is a [attractive/unattractive] industry because..."

## PESTLE Deep Dive

### The Six Factors
- **P**olitical — government policy, trade restrictions, political stability
- **E**conomic — GDP growth, interest rates, exchange rates, inflation
- **S**ocial — demographics, cultural trends, health consciousness, lifestyle
- **T**echnological — R&D, automation, technology incentives, innovation rate
- **L**egal — employment law, consumer protection, regulatory bodies
- **E**nvironmental — climate change, sustainability, waste management, carbon footprint

### When to Use Each Factor
Only include factors that actually affect the case. A tech startup case probably doesn't need deep Environmental analysis. A mining company case probably doesn't need deep Technological analysis.

## Financial Analysis Cheat Sheet

### Profitability
- Gross Margin = (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue
- Operating Margin = Operating Income / Revenue
- Net Margin = Net Income / Revenue
- ROE = Net Income / Shareholders' Equity
- ROA = Net Income / Total Assets

### Liquidity
- Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
- Quick Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities

### Leverage
- Debt-to-Equity = Total Debt / Total Equity
- Interest Coverage = EBIT / Interest Expense

### Growth
- Revenue CAGR over case period
- Market share trend

Always compare ratios to industry averages and historical trends — a 20% margin means nothing without context.
